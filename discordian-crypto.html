The Five Questions Nobody Wants to Answer

Every organization's cryptography policy starts the same way: "We use industry-standard, government-approved encryption algorithms..."

But here are the five questions you should be asking:

1. Who Approves These Algorithms?

Intelligence agencies. The same people running global surveillance programs and employing the world's best cryptanalysts.

2. What's Their Motivation?

To intercept communications. Their mission is signals intelligence‚Äîbreaking encryption is literally their job.

3. Can They Break What They Approve?

No. National security doctrine requires keeping capabilities secret. See: Dual_EC_DRBG, Crypto AG.

Use approved algorithms properly, layer defenses, and don't assume encryption alone protects you.

The Law of Fives never lies. There are always five uncomfortable questions about cryptography policy.

A Brief History of "Trust Us"

üìñ The Greatest Hits

Dual_EC_DRBG (2006-2013)

NSA-designed random number generator standardized by NIST. Turned out to have a mathematical backdoor that allowed NSA to predict "random" numbers and break encryption.

Status: Withdrawn after Snowden revelations. But it was "approved" for 7 years.

Crypto AG (1970-2018)

Swiss encryption company secretly owned by CIA and BND. Sold deliberately weakened encryption to governments worldwide for 50 years.

Impact: Entire countries used compromised "secure" communications for decades.

Export Restrictions (1990s)

US classified strong encryption as munitions to prevent export. Limited available crypto to 40-bit keys‚Äîeasily breakable even then.

Purpose: Ensure US intelligence could break most global encryption.

Clipper Chip (1993)

NSA-designed encryption chip with built-in law enforcement backdoor. Abandoned after public outcry‚Äîbut shows intent.

Lesson: When they can't backdoor standards, they try to backdoor implementations.

Question: Can we trust the same agencies to approve quantum-resistant standards?

What We Actually Do: Our Cryptography Policy

Given this uncomfortable reality, here's our public cryptography policy:

üéØ Five-Sided Defense Strategy

1. Use Modern Approved Algorithms

AES-256, RSA-4096, SHA-256 with proper implementation. Not because we trust them blindly‚Äîbut because breaking them is computationally expensive.

Rationale: Make surveillance expensive. Force them to use their best capabilities.

2. Prefer Non-NSA Alternatives

When available: ChaCha20, Ed25519, Argon2. Algorithms not designed by intelligence agencies may be more trustworthy.

Rationale: Diversify cryptographic dependencies.

3. Implementation Matters More Than Algorithm

Perfect crypto with bad implementation = useless. Use proven libraries (OpenSSL, libsodium, Go crypto). Don't roll your own.

Rationale: Most breaks are implementation flaws, not algorithmic.

4. Defense in Depth

Never rely on encryption alone. Layer: encryption + access control + monitoring + key rotation + audit logging.

Rationale: Assume crypto can be broken. Have other controls.

5. Transparency + Auditability

Public cryptography policy. Open-source tools when possible. Verify, don't trust.

Rationale: Transparency enables scrutiny‚Äîthe only real accountability.

Key Management: Where Everything Falls Apart

Here's the dirty secret: algorithm choice matters less than key management.

Perfect encryption is useless if:

  • Keys are stored in plaintext configuration files
  • Keys are never rotated
  • Keys are shared insecurely
  • Keys are accessible to too many people/systems
  • Key generation uses weak random number generators

This is where organizations fail. Not because NSA backdoored AES‚Äîbut because they left the keys in a text file in their S3 bucket.

üîë Our Key Management Principles

Hardware Security Modules (HSMs)

Use AWS KMS, Azure Key Vault, or dedicated HSMs for key storage. Keys never leave the secure boundary.

Automated Key Rotation

Rotate keys regularly: annually for master keys, quarterly for operational keys. Limit blast radius of compromise.

Principle of Least Privilege

Only systems/people that absolutely need key access get it. Use IAM policies, not shared credentials.

Audit Logging

Log every key use. Detect anomalous access patterns. Enable forensics after compromise.

Secure Key Generation

Use cryptographically secure RNGs. Never use predictable seeds. Test entropy sources.
